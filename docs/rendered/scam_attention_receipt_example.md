# Scam Attention Receipt: Three suspicious contacts in one afternoon

**Status:** VERIFY BEFORE ACTION — DO NOT CLICK, PAY, OR SHARE CODES  
**Scenario:** A person receives a bank text, a package-delivery SMS, and a boss-style gift-card email while trying to work.  
**Review owner:** Individual or household account owner  
**Last reviewed:** 2026-06-25  
**Receipt state:** ready_for_personal_use  

## Contact events
### Contact 1: SMS from Bank fraud department
- Requested action: Tap a link and enter login details to stop a suspicious transfer.
- Pressure tactic: Account-locked urgency and fear of losing money.
- Risk if true: A real account issue could need prompt attention through the bank's official channel.
- Risk if fake: Login details, one-time codes, or payment information could be stolen.
- Source of truth: Known bank app, card-back phone number, or official website typed manually.
- Notes: Do not use the SMS link or reply thread.
### Contact 2: SMS from Package delivery company
- Requested action: Pay a small redelivery fee through a shortened link.
- Pressure tactic: Small fee plus delivery deadline.
- Risk if true: A real delivery may need rescheduling through the carrier account.
- Risk if fake: Card information and address details could be captured.
- Source of truth: Carrier app, merchant order page, or tracking number typed into the official carrier site.
- Notes: Check whether a package is actually expected.
### Contact 3: Email from Manager
- Requested action: Buy gift cards quickly and send photos of the codes.
- Pressure tactic: Authority, secrecy, and hurry.
- Risk if true: A real manager request should still follow purchasing rules.
- Risk if fake: Gift-card codes are effectively cash and usually cannot be recovered.
- Source of truth: Known internal chat, direct call to the manager, or finance/purchasing policy.
- Notes: Never send gift-card codes by email.

## Verification route
- Pause before responding; screenshot or save the message without clicking its links.
- Use a known-good route: official app, bookmarked site, card-back number, internal directory, or in-person confirmation.
- Check whether the requested action matches normal policy or past behavior.
- If money, login, identity, codes, downloads, or secrecy are involved, escalate to the relevant owner before acting.

## Do not do until verified
- Do not click links in the suspicious message.
- Do not share one-time codes, passwords, gift-card codes, identity numbers, or payment details.
- Do not call phone numbers supplied only inside the suspicious message.
- Do not let urgency substitute for a known source of truth.

## Safe next step
Verify the highest-risk message first through a known-good route, then block/report/delete only after preserving any evidence needed for the account owner or workplace process.

## Boundary notes
- Synthetic example only; do not paste real scam messages containing private information into public tools.
- This is not legal, banking, cybersecurity, or identity-theft recovery advice.
- Emergency threats, financial loss, account compromise, and identity exposure require official reporting routes.

## Boundary
This receipt does not determine that a contact is safe or fraudulent. It is a local attention-and-verification aid. For real fraud, financial loss, identity exposure, workplace threats, account compromise, legal issues, or emergency claims, use official reporting and domain-owner procedures.

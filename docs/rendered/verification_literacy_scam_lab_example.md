# Verification Literacy Mini-Lab: Scam messages as a verification-literacy drill

**Status:** ready_for_classroom_or_workshop_review  
**Scenario:** A 25-minute workshop uses three synthetic messages to practice source-chain checks without clicking suspicious links.  
**Audience:** Students, families, staff teams, community groups, or small organizations  
**Facilitator:** Teacher, librarian, manager, parent, or local workshop lead  
**Last reviewed:** 2026-06-25  
**Duration:** 20–30 minutes  

## Learning goal
Participants learn to separate surface plausibility from source verification, identify urgency tactics, choose known-good verification routes, and explain how confidence changes after checking.

## Synthetic messages
### Message 1
> Your bank account has been locked. Verify your identity immediately at the link below or your transfer will process.

- Surface cues to notice:
- Urgency
- money risk
- link supplied by the message
- identity request
- Possible source of truth: Known bank app, card-back phone number, or official site typed manually.
- Safe action: Do not use the link; verify through the official bank route.
- Confidence before verification: Low. It could name a real bank, but the verification route is unsafe.
- Confidence after verification: Higher only after checking the official app/site/number; otherwise treat as unverified.
### Message 2
> We could not deliver your package. Pay $1.92 to reschedule today.

- Surface cues to notice:
- Tiny fee
- deadline
- delivery context
- payment request
- Possible source of truth: Merchant order page, carrier app, or tracking number typed into the official carrier site.
- Safe action: Check whether a package is expected and verify through the official carrier route.
- Confidence before verification: Medium-low if a package is expected; low if no package is expected.
- Confidence after verification: Depends on whether the official carrier system shows a matching shipment and fee.
### Message 3
> I am in a meeting. Buy four gift cards for a client and send me the codes. Need this in 20 minutes.

- Surface cues to notice:
- Authority
- secrecy
- gift-card codes
- short deadline
- Possible source of truth: Known manager phone/chat, finance policy, or purchasing system.
- Safe action: Verify with the person through a known channel and follow purchasing policy; do not send codes.
- Confidence before verification: Low because the requested payment method and secrecy are abnormal.
- Confidence after verification: Only verified if the known manager and purchasing process confirm it; gift-card code requests should remain suspect.

## Verification steps
- Name the requested action before reacting.
- Identify the pressure tactic: urgency, fear, authority, money, secrecy, convenience, or social proof.
- Choose a known-good source of truth that does not come from the suspicious message.
- Decide what evidence to preserve: screenshot, sender address, timestamp, claimed amount, or account affected.
- Update confidence after checking; do not confuse confidence with certainty.

## Reflection prompts
- Which message felt most plausible before checking, and why?
- What made a verification route safer than clicking the supplied link?
- Where does scam pressure create a tax on attention, time, and trust?
- What rule would help a household, classroom, or team respond calmly next time?

## Boundary notes / safe-use boundary
- Use synthetic messages only; do not ask participants to click or visit suspicious links.
- Do not collect private scam messages, account numbers, phone numbers, passwords, or one-time codes in the lab.
- This is not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or identity-theft recovery advice.
- Escalate real threats, losses, account compromise, identity exposure, or emergency claims through official routes.

## Boundary
This mini-lab labels confidence and safer verification routes. It does not certify truth, investigate live fraud, provide legal/financial/cybersecurity advice, or replace official reporting and recovery procedures.

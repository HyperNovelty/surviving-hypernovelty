## Candidate review cards

### 1) Shadow AI Amnesty Intake Card
- Lane: repo/free; paid later for team facilitation or policy rollout support.
- Adaptation burden revealed: workers are adopting AI faster than official policy and approved tooling can adapt; hidden use creates data, accountability, and resentment risk.
- Who carries it: frontline staff, managers, IT/security, HR/L&D, and compliance owners.
- Missing layer: a psychologically safe intake primitive that separates “tell us what helps” from punishment, then routes uses into allowed, needs guardrails, pilot, or stop.
- Smallest proof artifact: one-page form with fields for task, tool, data sensitivity, output destination, decision impact, frequency, why official workflow failed, and requested safe alternative.
- Genericity-test risk: medium; generic policy prompts can draft rules, but the value is a repeatable intake workflow plus examples that reduce disclosure fear.
- Safety/claim caveats: not legal/HR advice; should not invite confession of illegal activity or require sensitive details in unsafe channels.
- Next build step: add a Markdown template and three fictional examples: school staff, nonprofit operations, small business sales support.

### 2) Agent Handoff Receipt
- Lane: repo/free; paid later for implementation support in agentic workflows.
- Adaptation burden revealed: as agents become workflow actors, humans inherit outputs without knowing what the agent saw, changed, assumed, skipped, or escalated.
- Who carries it: operations leads, support teams, analysts, editors, managers, and whoever signs off after automation.
- Missing layer: a compact handoff receipt between agent/tool and human checkpoint.
- Smallest proof artifact: JSON/Markdown receipt with input sources, actions taken, tools called, files/accounts touched, uncertainties, rejected options, human decision required, and rollback path.
- Genericity-test risk: low-medium; stronger if paired with existing Agent Permission Receipt and Toolchain Exposure Map schemas.
- Safety/claim caveats: visibility aid only; does not prove the agent behaved correctly or securely unless logs are independently verified.
- Next build step: create a template that composes with `/tools/agent-permission-receipt-card/` and `/tools/agent-toolchain-exposure-map/`.

### 3) AI Literacy Freshness Ledger
- Lane: Future of Education + institution/trust pack; repo/free first.
- Adaptation burden revealed: AI literacy cannot be a fixed course because model capabilities, interfaces, and risks shift every few months.
- Who carries it: teachers, workforce trainers, students, employees, curriculum owners, and administrators.
- Missing layer: a curriculum freshness ledger that tracks which AI examples, policies, and assignments are stale.
- Smallest proof artifact: table with lesson/module, assumption taught, what changed, source/date checked, risk if stale, replacement activity, owner, next review date.
- Genericity-test risk: low-medium; value comes from recurring review cadence and specific stale-assumption categories.
- Safety/claim caveats: avoid claiming curriculum adequacy or compliance; local educators must approve content.
- Next build step: build a fictional 4-week “AI at Work” course freshness diff example.

### 4) Proof-of-Learning Conference Card
- Lane: Future of Education; repo/free; paid later for teacher/school implementation kit.
- Adaptation burden revealed: detector-first academic integrity creates false certainty; educators need short human verification rituals that do not become surveillance.
- Who carries it: students, teachers, parents, tutors, academic integrity staff.
- Missing layer: a 5–10 minute oral/interactive conference template tied to a student’s evidence packet.
- Smallest proof artifact: conference script with three explain-your-work prompts, one transfer prompt, one misconception prompt, allowed-AI disclosure, and teacher notes.
- Genericity-test risk: medium; must be more structured than “ask the student questions” by connecting to dossier artifacts and rubrics.
- Safety/claim caveats: not proof of non-AI authorship; supports learning judgment, not forensic authorship detection.
- Next build step: add a worked example that uses the existing Assessment Evidence Packet Lite fields.

### 5) Synthetic Media Response Ladder
- Lane: media/trust pack + HN article; monitor for paid only with institutional partner.
- Adaptation burden revealed: deepfakes and synthetic political/media artifacts force ordinary people and institutions to decide before verification ecosystems catch up.
- Who carries it: voters, journalists, schools, civic groups, communications staff, public agencies, families.
- Missing layer: a response ladder for suspicious media: pause, preserve, source-trace, cross-check, context-check, escalation, publish/share decision.
- Smallest proof artifact: printable checklist for a suspicious image/video/audio/message, with “do not amplify while verifying” defaults.
- Genericity-test risk: medium-high; needs concrete escalation thresholds and examples to avoid generic media literacy.
- Safety/claim caveats: do not claim deepfake detection accuracy; avoid naming private people or spreading unverified allegations.
- Next build step: create a fictional campaign/video rumor example and a nonpolitical school-closure rumor example.

### 6) Answer-Layer Claim Patch Note
- Lane: iPublishOS + repo/free; paid later for publisher workflow customization.

- Missing layer: a page-level “claim patch note” that makes changes, dates, evidence, and superseded statements easy for humans and answer layers to inspect.
- Smallest proof artifact: Markdown block: claim ID, current wording, prior wording, why changed, evidence/source, date, maintainer, citation snippet, caveats.
- Genericity-test risk: low if integrated with existing Answer-Layer Citation Readiness Card; high if framed as SEO/GEO magic.
- Safety/claim caveats: no ranking/citation/traffic promises; do not imply answer engines will respect the patch note.

### 7) Human Recourse Map
- Lane: Human Premium + institution/trust pack; paid later for service design review.

- Who carries it: patients, students, clients, applicants, customers, frontline staff, complaint handlers.

- Genericity-test risk: medium; many accountability checklists exist, but the Human Premium framing can make it concrete for service surfaces.

### 10) Source Substitution Alert
- Lane: media/trust + iPublishOS; monitor/free; paid only for editorial workflow support.
- Adaptation burden revealed: AI summaries and content farms can replace original sources with derivative or lower-quality citations, weakening evidence chains.
- Who carries it: readers, students, editors, researchers, publishers, institutional communicators.
- Missing layer: a checklist that flags when a claim’s source has been substituted from primary/original to derivative/AI/summary/social repost.
- Smallest proof artifact: claim-source table with original source, current cited source, transformation type, missing context, confidence, and repair action.
- Genericity-test risk: medium; must be more than source evaluation by focusing on substitution over time.
- Safety/claim caveats: do not label sources fraudulent without evidence; frame as chain-of-custody hygiene.
- Next build step: extend Source Confidence Ledger with a “source lineage/substitution” example.

## Candidate review cards

### 1) Shadow AI Disclosure Ladder
- Lane: repo/free; paid later for team rollout; monitor workplace governance.
- Adaptation burden revealed: workers are using unapproved tools because official tools/policies lag real workflows; treating this only as misconduct hides useful workflow intelligence.
- Who carries it: employees, managers, HR, security, IT, operations leads, new/early-career workers under adoption pressure.
- Missing layer: a low-friction disclosure path that distinguishes harmless experimentation, sensitive-data exposure, workflow dependency, and urgent security risk.
- Smallest proof artifact: one-page ladder: “I used AI for…” / data touched / output used where / policy uncertainty / remediation needed / no-blame escalation route.
- Genericity-test risk: medium; many AI policies mention disclosure, so value must come from the graded ladder, worker-safe language, and linkage to AI Use Clarity Micro-Policy + Agent Permission Receipt.
- Safety/claim caveats: not legal, HR, or security advice; cannot promise amnesty; must be adapted by authorized policy owners before institutional use.
- Next build step: create a synthetic team example with three disclosures: harmless drafting, accidental confidential paste, and recurring workaround dependency.

### 2) AI Workflow Value Evidence Log
- Lane: paid later; repo/free primitive first; Human Premium/work pack.
- Adaptation burden revealed: organizations are pressured to prove AI ROI while actual gains are hidden by review time, rework, role shifts, and downstream bottlenecks.
- Who carries it: team leads, operations managers, CFO/COO staff, transformation offices, frontline workers whose work is being measured.
- Missing layer: humble before/after evidence that separates claimed productivity from observed time, quality, review cost, errors, and trust effects.
- Smallest proof artifact: Markdown/CSV schema: use case, baseline time, AI-assisted time, human review time, rework/error rate, quality signal, risk introduced, downstream bottleneck, worker burden, confidence.
- Genericity-test risk: low-medium if it forces evidence and caveats; high if it becomes generic ROI theater.
- Safety/claim caveats: avoid productivity guarantees; do not use for worker surveillance without explicit safeguards; self-reported data should be labeled weak.
- Next build step: build a fictional 4-week support-team log plus a “claim not yet supported” checklist.

### 3) Non-Human Identity Review Receipt
- Lane: repo/free; paid later for implementation support; monitor agentic IAM.
- Adaptation burden revealed: agentic tools act through service accounts, API keys, OAuth apps, browser profiles, and tool permissions faster than old access-review rituals can track.
- Who carries it: IT/security, ops owners, managers, tool admins, compliance, anyone accountable for agent actions.
- Missing layer: a short periodic review receipt for one non-human identity: what it can read/write/spend/publish, what changed, where logs live, who owns it, when it expires.
- Smallest proof artifact: receipt template that extends Agent Identity & Scope Roster with review date, delta since last review, permission evidence, revoke/renew decision, and unresolved gaps.
- Genericity-test risk: low because concrete fields + expiry/review evidence are more useful than a generic prompt.
- Safety/claim caveats: not a security certification or IAM replacement; never store secrets; sensitive environments require professional security review.
- Next build step: add a synthetic quarterly review for a “grant-drafting agent,” “CRM enrichment bot,” and “website update agent.”

- Genericity-test risk: medium; must avoid becoming a generic admin binder by composing existing Hypernovelty artifacts.
- Safety/claim caveats: not legal/compliance advice; local policy authority required; avoid tool-certification claims.

### 5) Teacher Time-to-Learn Budget Planner

### 6) Verification Literacy Mini-Lab
- Lane: repo/free; Future of Education; media/trust; HN article.
- Adaptation burden revealed: people can no longer rely on appearance, polish, screenshots, or fluent summaries as trust signals; verification literacy is becoming a basic civic/work skill.
- Who carries it: students, readers, workers, community groups, librarians, educators, managers.
- Missing layer: a 20–30 minute local exercise that teaches how a claim becomes trustworthy: source chain, evidence type, update date, incentive, confidence, and safe-use boundary.
- Smallest proof artifact: mini-lab packet with one claim, three source variants, verification steps, confidence before/after, and reflection prompt.
- Genericity-test risk: medium; generic media-literacy lessons exist, so value comes from compact receipts that plug into Source Confidence Ledger and Platformized News Diet Receipt.
- Safety/claim caveats: labels confidence, not truth; should avoid partisan framing and unsupported accusations of motive.
- Next build step: produce a synthetic lab around an AI-health-tip claim and an AI-education-policy claim.

### 7) Answer-Layer Source Substitution Watch
- Lane: iPublishOS; media/trust; monitor; paid later for recurring review.

- Who carries it: small publishers, researchers, nonprofits, public-information teams, educators, niche experts.

- Safety/claim caveats: no guarantee of ranking, citation, traffic, or revenue; avoid scraping against ToS; use manual review or approved APIs only.
- Next build step: create a synthetic watch for “AI use policy for community college assignments” and “hypernovelty adaptation debt.”

### 8) Human Recourse Path Card
- Lane: Human Premium; repo/free; paid later for service redesign support.
- Adaptation burden revealed: as AI handles intake, support, triage, and advice, people need to know how to reach a human, challenge an output, or repair harm.
- Who carries it: customers, students, patients/clients, support staff, service designers, managers.
- Missing layer: a visible recourse card attached to any AI-supported workflow: what the AI can decide, what it cannot decide, how to appeal, response-time expectation, and human owner.
- Smallest proof artifact: card template with “if this feels wrong,” “human review trigger,” “what to include,” “who receives it,” “expected timeline,” and “no retaliation/no penalty note” where appropriate.
- Genericity-test risk: low-medium because it concretizes the Human Premium trust surface; generic if not attached to real workflow examples.
- Safety/claim caveats: not a legal appeals process; timelines/rights must be approved locally; high-stakes sectors need specialist review.
- Next build step: create examples for student financial-aid chatbot, healthcare scheduling bot, and HR benefits assistant.
